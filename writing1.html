Taught the Boys How to Play Baseball

Inspired by "The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao" by Junot Diaz

Posted by Caitlin on October 5, 2014

It’s your first day of baseball. As you walk through the gate, you can already smell the packed dirt, the chalk base lines, the leather of a dozen mitts. Your dad’s fingers are wrapped around yours as you make the trip to the dugout, where several other five and six year olds have lined up. He carries the duffel bag you packed so carefully the night before, while your mom follows close behind with enough camera equipment to film a movie. That morning you had laid out everything you would need on the floor of your room, your mom checking items off of the list:

Mitt? Check

Uniform? Check

Sneakers? Check

Water? Check

The entire five years of your life have been spent preparing for this moment. Running after your baby brother in the playground. Playing catch on those late summer evenings. Watching the Mets actually win games at Shea Stadium.

Walking into the dugout, all you can see are boys: boys with their parents bent over their feet, tying their shoes; boys sticking their hands into their new gloves; boys staring unabashedly at the plastic bag of snacks stashed under the bench. At the end of the bench, a solitary girl sits, pulling her baseball hat carefully over her ponytail. You meet her eyes, smile, and rush to grab the seat next to her.

You’re ready to play ball.

* * * *

As a six year old, your hair is very important. Some girls wear it down, ornamented with headbands, barrettes, and ribbons of various colors. Others choose to put their hair into smooth braids, but there is a stark difference between the one-braiders, the two-braiders, and the corn rowers. The corn rowers have intense hair, their heads weighed down by dozens of thin, little braids each finished with a bead. Then there are the ponytails.

You wear your hair in a ponytail, but it’s not a typical ponytail—you didn’t brush through your hair for three seconds, wrap a rubber band around it, and call it a day. Your ponytail is a meticulous, time-consuming project…for your mom at least.

You spent that particular Saturday morning perched on the edge of one of the dining room chairs as your mom ran the brush through your hair, eventually tying it up into a ponytail. This process was repeated several times until the ponytail met your standards. Normally, your ponytail needed to be smooth to the touch. You didn’t want to find a stray hair when, during the day, you decided to run your hand over your hair. But today was especially important, because you were going to ballet. Your mom had to do, redo, and redo your hair before its quality was ensured, given the see-if-it-falls-off-if-I-shake-my-head-really-hard stamp of approval.

Leotard? Check

Tights? Check

Slippers? Check

Hair? Double check

By the time you reached your ballet class, your stomach felt like it was about to explode from all of the butterflies you’d swallowed on the bus ride. Practicing arabesques and pliés in front of the parallel-universe-class you saw in the mirror was no sweat (just a lot of muscle pain afterwards). But performing the same exact routine with the same exact people in the same exact room with the same exact background music in front of a group of parents with their index fingers glued to their cameras and video cameras? Horrifying.

Back straight, toes pointed, arms above your head. Chin out, eyes ahead, smile wide. Stay in your line. Listen to the music, feel the beat, hear your teacher’s fervent snaps. 5, 6, 7, 8, go. Point your toe now. Twirl here. Plié, releve, and stand. Arabesque. Up on your toes, twirl. Face the audience and curtsy.

A smattering of applause erupts from the rows of parents, camera shutters clicking away to get the perfect shot of their perfect daughter curtsying perfectly.

* * * *

“2, 4, 6, 8, who do we appreciate? Flyingpurplepeopleeaters! Flyingpurplepeopleeaters! Go Flyingpurplepeopleeaters!” You grimace as the other team’s hands fly up into the air, “appreciating” your team. It was not your decision to name your team after a popular Halloween song. The stars on the sleeves of your purple soccer jersey spoke of the Purple Stars, or the Shooting Stars, or a team name that did not consist of four random words.

Despite the effort the other team put into rushing through the name, it still didn’t deaden the thump of victory in your ears. Adrenaline still running high, people high-fived each other left and right on the way to the trophy ceremony. It was a crushing win for the Flying Purple People Eaters, nothing new. As the only team with two fifth graders, the Flying Purple People Eaters had a huge advantage over the wimpy fourth grade teams.

The rest of the girls look up to you, starting conversations as you walk with them to the coach. A box of trophies at his feet, the chatter dies down and he begins to hand out the cheap flags the soccer league had used instead of trophies this year. With “FLYING PURPLE PEOPLE EATERS” emblazoned in huge letters, the ceremony has no personalization, no mini speech for each player. The team acts like an assembly line: left hand, flag; right hand, team photos; stand next to coach for a few seconds while your parents frantically snap pictures on their smartphones.

You remember a former teammate of yours, Rachel. Whenever someone got injured, whether they were on your team on not, she would go down on one knee, head bent, hands clapping. During the first few years of soccer, by the end of the game, everyone’s knees would be disgusting, covered in cuts and bruises from stealing the ball or falling on your face, caked in dusty mud. As the years passed, the girls got older, the games fell into a routine, and the knees were no longer dirty.

* * * *

It’s your first day of softball. As you walk through the gate, you can already smell the packed dirt, the chalk base lines, the leather of a dozen mitts. Your dad’s fingers are wrapped around your little sister’s as you make the trip to the dugout, where several other nine and ten year olds have lined up. He carries the duffel bag you packed so carefully the next before, while your mom follows close behind with enough camera equipment to film a movie. That morning you had laid out everything you would need on the floor of your room, your mom checking items off of the list:

Mitt? Check

Uniform? Check

Sneakers? Check

Water? Check

The entire ten years of your life have been spent preparing for this moment. Working on your flexibility and agility in ballet. Practicing yelling at each other from across the field. Being the best on a team consisting of all boys, except for you.

Walking into the dugout, all you can see are girls: girls unloading their bats and gloves; girls rolling softballs around in their palms; girls gathered in groups, talking about their plans for the summer. At the end of the bench, a group of new girls sit, pulling their visors carefully over their ponytails. They all look up at the sound of your sneakers smacking against the concrete floor of the dugout and smile. You rush over to their group, bag in hand, an introduction already rolling off of your tongue.

You’re ready to play ball.

It’s June, three months after the first day. By now, you know all of the boys on the team. You aren’t “girl #1”—you’re just another player on the Coronas. The hours you’ve spent fielding, batting, catching, throwing, yelling at people, getting yelled at—they all culminate to this very moment: trophies.

Coach Ken stands in the middle of the field, a small circle of players and parents forming around him. He pulls out a trophy at random. “First up is, Nick!” The speech begins to flow, peppered with laughter and anecdotes about Nick’s season on the Coronas, and ends with a trophy being handed, a photo being taken, and applause all around.

The cycle continues. One by one, the players stand up, listen to their speeches, and take their trophies, eagerly clutching them to their chests. It’s finally down to you and Caroline, the other girl on the team.

“Last, but most certainly not least, are the two most important players on this team: Caitlin and Caroline.” A smile is plastered onto your face and you take your place next to Coach. He smiles down at you.

“They taught the boys how to play baseball.”
